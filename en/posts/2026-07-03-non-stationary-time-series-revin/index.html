Taming Non-stationary Time Series: Reversible Normalization against Drift and Exponential Growth

Data Science
Artificial Intelligence
Time Series
Non-stationary Time Series
Reversible Normalization
RevIN

When a series’ mean and variance drift over time, a model trained on the past meets a test set it has never seen the scale of. This article explains non-stationarity and concept drift from the ground up and builds — with runnable code — from naive normalization to Reversible Instance Normalization (RevIN).

Prof. Shin

1. The problem: a model that trained on one world and is tested in another

Most machine learning rests on a quiet assumption: the data you train on and the data you predict on are drawn from the same distribution. Time series routinely break that assumption. A series is stationary if its statistical properties — mean, variance, autocorrelation — stay constant over time. Real series are usually non-stationary: sales grow, prices inflate, sensors drift, a product goes viral and the level jumps. The statistics you measured on the training window no longer describe the window you are forecasting.

This has a specific, damaging consequence for forecasting, and it is worth stating precisely before writing any model. If a model learns a mapping from an input window to a future window, and the scale of those windows keeps rising, then at test time the model receives inputs numerically unlike anything in training. It is being asked to extrapolate, not interpolate. Let us make the phenomenon concrete with a synthetic series that has all three troubles at once: an exponential trend, abrupt level shifts (a stand-in for concept drift), and noise whose size grows with the level (heteroscedasticity).

import numpy as np
np.random.seed(0)

def make_series(N=2400):
    """Synthetic non-stationary series with three sources of drift:
    - exp(0.0028 t): exponential trend (multiplicative growth)
    - level shifts at t=600,1200,1800: abrupt regime changes (concept drift)
    - noise scaled by sqrt(trend): heteroscedastic (variance grows over time)"""
    t = np.arange(N)
    trend = np.exp(0.0028 * t)
    season = 0.4 * np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 48) * np.sqrt(trend)
    level = np.zeros(N)
    for cp in [600, 1200, 1800]:
        level[cp:] += np.random.uniform(2, 5) * np.sqrt(trend[cp])
    noise = 0.08 * np.random.randn(N) * trend
    return (trend + season + level + noise).astype(np.float32)

series = make_series()

# Quantify the non-stationarity: statistics per third of the series.
for i, part in enumerate(np.array_split(series, 3)):
    print(f"third {i+1}: mean={part.mean():8.2f}  std={part.std():7.2f}")
third 1: mean=    5.84  std=   5.80
third 2: mean=   54.65  std=  31.71
third 3: mean=  395.70  std= 218.94

The three thirds of the series have wildly different means and standard deviations — the mean grows by more than an order of magnitude from the first third to the last. A model that sees only the early portion during training has no way to know the later portion will live at a completely different scale. This is the failure that normalization must address.

2. Naming the two failures: distribution shift and concept drift

Two terms describe what is going wrong, and keeping them distinct helps you choose a fix.

Distribution shift is the umbrella term: the joint distribution of the data changes between training and deployment. In time series this shows up most visibly as a change in the marginal statistics — the mean and variance of the values themselves move over time (Kim et al. 2022). The exponential trend in the code above is pure distribution shift: nothing about the relationship between past and future changed, only the scale.

Concept drift is the sharper, and more dangerous, cousin. Here the relationship between inputs and target — the underlying function you are trying to learn — changes over time. A level shift caused by a new competitor entering the market, a regulation, or a pandemic is concept drift: the rule that generated the data before the change no longer generates it after. Recent work formalizes that in forecasting this drift has two distinct faces: it can shift within the space of input windows over time (intra-space shift), and it can differ between the input window and the horizon window you are predicting (inter-space shift) (Fan et al. 2023). The second is easy to overlook and is exactly what makes forecasting harder than ordinary supervised learning: even a single training example spans two possibly-different distributions, its lookback and its horizon.

Normalization is the first line of defense against the distribution-shift part. The rest of this article builds up the normalization toolkit in order of sophistication, measuring each step.

3. First attempt: global normalization, and why it fails

The reflex from tabular ML is to standardize: subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation, computed once over the training set. This global normalization stabilizes the numeric range for optimization, but it does nothing about drift, because a single fixed mean and variance cannot describe a series whose mean and variance are moving targets. Subtracting the training mean from a test window that lives ten times higher leaves a huge residual the model was never trained on.

We need the supervised machinery in place to measure this, so let us set up windowing (respecting time order — no shuffling, no leakage) and a small MLP forecaster.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
torch.manual_seed(0)

L, H = 48, 24    # lookback length, forecast horizon

def make_windows(x, L, H):
    """Turn a 1-D series into (X, Y): X is (n, L) lookback, Y is (n, H) horizon.
    Windows are built in time order; window i never contains future information."""
    X, Y = [], []
    for i in range(L, len(x) - H + 1):
        X.append(x[i - L:i])
        Y.append(x[i:i + H])
    return np.stack(X), np.stack(Y)

X, Y = make_windows(series, L, H)
n = len(X)
split = int(n * 0.7)                       # first 70% train, last 30% test (chronological)
Xtr, Ytr, Xte, Yte = X[:split], Y[:split], X[split:], Y[split:]

class MLP(nn.Module):
    """A minimal forecaster: L inputs -> 128 hidden -> H outputs."""
    def __init__(self, L, H):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(nn.Linear(L, 128), nn.ReLU(), nn.Linear(128, H))
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(x)

print(f"train windows: {Xtr.shape}, test windows: {Xte.shape}")
print(f"train input mean: {Xtr.mean():.1f}, test input mean: {Xte.mean():.1f} "
      f"({Xte.mean()/Xtr.mean():.1f}x higher)")
train windows: (1630, 48), test windows: (699, 48)
train input mean: 33.8, test input mean: 385.6 (11.4x higher)

The last line is the whole problem in one number: the test windows sit at roughly an order of magnitude higher level than the training windows. Any model, and any global normalization fit on the training set, is now extrapolating.

4. The idea that works: normalize each instance, then put it back

The key move, introduced as Reversible Instance Normalization (RevIN), is to stop normalizing globally and start normalizing per instance — and, crucially, to make the normalization reversible (Kim et al. 2022). For each input window, you remove that window’s own mean and standard deviation before the model sees it, so every window arrives at the model looking stationary regardless of what absolute level it came from. Then, after the model produces its (normalized) forecast, you add the window’s statistics back to return the prediction to the original scale.

Formally, for an input window \(x \in \mathbb{R}^{L}\) (one channel), RevIN computes the instance statistics

\[ \mu_x = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{t=1}^{L} x_t, \qquad \sigma_x^2 = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{t=1}^{L}(x_t - \mu_x)^2, \tag{1}\]

normalizes with a learnable affine transform (parameters \(\gamma, \beta\)),

\[ \hat{x}_t = \gamma \, \frac{x_t - \mu_x}{\sqrt{\sigma_x^2 + \epsilon}} + \beta, \tag{2}\]

feeds \(\hat{x}\) through the model to get a normalized forecast \(\hat{y}\), and then denormalizes by inverting Equation 2 with the same \(\mu_x, \sigma_x\):

\[ y_t = \sqrt{\sigma_x^2 + \epsilon}\;\cdot\;\frac{\hat{y}_t - \beta}{\gamma} + \mu_x. \tag{3}\]

Here \(\epsilon\) is a small constant for numerical stability, and \(\gamma, \beta \in \mathbb{R}\) are learned. The symmetry is the point: the non-stationary information (the level and scale of each window) is removed at the input and restored at the output, so the model only ever has to learn the stationary shape, not the moving level (Kim et al. 2022).

Before measuring accuracy, it is worth seeing directly what the normalization does to the input distribution. This is the conceptual heart of the method:

def windows_only(x, L):
    return np.stack([x[i - L:i] for i in range(L, len(x) - L)])

W = windows_only(series, L)
raw_means = W.mean(axis=1)
raw_stds = W.std(axis=1)

# Per-instance normalization (RevIN's removal step), no affine:
Wn = (W - W.mean(axis=1, keepdims=True)) / (W.std(axis=1, keepdims=True) + 1e-8)

print("RAW windows    : mean in [%.1f, %.1f], std in [%.1f, %.1f]"
      % (raw_means.min(), raw_means.max(), raw_stds.min(), raw_stds.max()))
print("RevIN-normed   : mean in [%.3f, %.3f], std in [%.3f, %.3f]"
      % (Wn.mean(axis=1).min(), Wn.mean(axis=1).max(),
         Wn.std(axis=1).min(), Wn.std(axis=1).max()))
print("\nWindow means spanned a %.0fx range; RevIN collapses them to ~0."
      % (raw_means.max() / raw_means.min()))
RAW windows    : mean in [1.1, 753.1], std in [0.3, 53.8]
RevIN-normed   : mean in [-0.000, 0.000], std in [1.000, 1.000]

Window means spanned a 702x range; RevIN collapses them to ~0.

The raw window means span a range of several hundred to one; after per-instance normalization every window has mean ~0 and standard deviation ~1. From the model’s point of view the drifting series has been turned into a stationary one. That is what lets a model trained on the low-level early data still work on the high-level late data — the level was never the model’s problem to solve.

5. Measuring it: three strategies head to head

Now we train the same MLP three ways — no normalization, global normalization, and RevIN-style per-instance normalization with denormalization — and compare. The training loop is deliberately explicit so each strategy’s normalization is visible.

def rmse(a, b):
    return float(np.sqrt(((a - b) ** 2).mean()))

def run(mode, epochs=120):
    torch.manual_seed(0)
    Xt, Yt, Xv = torch.tensor(Xtr), torch.tensor(Ytr), torch.tensor(Xte)
    if mode == "global":
        m, sd = Xt.mean(), Xt.std()
    model = MLP(L, H)
    opt = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3)
    lossf = nn.MSELoss()
    for _ in range(epochs):
        model.train()
        if mode == "revin":
            mu = Xt.mean(1, keepdim=True); sg = Xt.std(1, keepdim=True) + 1e-5
            xin, ytar = (Xt - mu) / sg, (Yt - mu) / sg   # normalize input AND target per instance
        elif mode == "global":
            xin, ytar = (Xt - m) / sd, (Yt - m) / sd
        else:  # none
            xin, ytar = Xt, Yt
        opt.zero_grad()
        loss = lossf(model(xin), ytar)
        loss.backward()
        opt.step()
    model.eval()
    with torch.no_grad():
        if mode == "revin":
            mu = Xv.mean(1, keepdim=True); sg = Xv.std(1, keepdim=True) + 1e-5
            pred = (model((Xv - mu) / sg) * sg + mu).numpy()   # denormalize back to scale
        elif mode == "global":
            pred = (model((Xv - m) / sd) * sd + m).numpy()
        else:
            pred = model(Xv).numpy()
    return pred

preds = {mode: run(mode) for mode in ["none", "global", "revin"]}
print("absolute test RMSE (dominated by the large-scale test region):")
for mode, p in preds.items():
    print(f"  {mode:8s} {rmse(p, Yte):8.3f}")
absolute test RMSE (dominated by the large-scale test region):
  none       33.316
  global     34.634
  revin      34.112

The absolute RMSE numbers are close, and that itself is a lesson worth reading carefully rather than glossing over. Two things are happening. First, absolute RMSE on this test set is dominated by the handful of very-high-scale windows at the end, so it is a poor lens for comparing strategies — a large error on a large-scale window swamps everything else. Second, a plain MLP with a ReLU layer can itself learn a surprising amount of scaling, so on smooth synthetic drift the gap to “no normalization” is small. To compare fairly we need a scale-free metric: divide each window’s error by that window’s own level, so a 5% error counts the same whether the window sits at 10 or at 500.

def relative_rmse(pred, true, Xwin):
    """Per-window RMSE divided by the window's mean level (a MASE-like, scale-free score)."""
    scale = np.abs(Xwin).mean(axis=1, keepdims=True) + 1e-8
    per_window = np.sqrt(((pred - true) ** 2).mean(axis=1, keepdims=True)) / scale
    return float(per_window.mean())

print("scale-free relative RMSE (lower is better):")
scores = {}
for mode, p in preds.items():
    scores[mode] = relative_rmse(p, Yte, Xte)
    print(f"  {mode:8s} {scores[mode]:.4f}")

print(f"\nRevIN vs global normalization: "
      f"{(scores['global'] - scores['revin']) / scores['global'] * 100:+.1f}%")
scale-free relative RMSE (lower is better):
  none     0.0805
  global   0.0829
  revin    0.0804

RevIN vs global normalization: +3.0%

On the scale-free metric RevIN edges out global normalization by a few percent, while global normalization is actually the worst of the three here — subtracting a single fixed mean from windows that live far above it is worse than doing nothing. This modest, honest gap is characteristic: on clean synthetic data with a cooperative model the effect is real but small. The published case for RevIN is built on real datasets and stronger backbones, where removing per-instance level before a Transformer or linear model yields substantial and consistent gains across horizons (Kim et al. 2022); a simple linear model wrapped in RevIN has been reported to match or beat far heavier models on standard benchmarks.

6. Knowing the limits: when removing the level removes the signal

RevIN is not free, and the reason is conceptually important. By stripping each window’s mean and variance, you are asserting that the level and scale are nuisance — information to be removed and restored, not used. Sometimes that assertion is wrong. If a rising level or a change in variance is itself predictive of the future — if the trend persists into the horizon in a way the stationary shape cannot capture — then normalizing it away discards signal.

This is the critique behind the Non-stationary Transformer, which observes that naive stationarization can over-stationarize: it can erase precisely the non-stationary events the model needs to distinguish, so the authors pair stationarization with a “de-stationary attention” that re-injects the removed statistics back into the model’s internals rather than only at the output (Liu et al. 2022). It is also the motivation for Dish-TS, which points out that RevIN implicitly assumes the input window and the horizon window share statistics — that the mean you remove from the lookback is the right mean to restore on the horizon. When the input and output spaces have genuinely different distributions (inter-space shift), a single set of statistics is not enough, and Dish-TS learns separate distribution coefficients for the input and output spaces (Fan et al. 2023).

The practical reading: instance normalization is a strong, cheap default that fixes the common case (drifting level), but it encodes an assumption — level and scale are nuisance, and lookback statistics predict horizon statistics — that you should check against your data rather than accept blindly.

7. Conclusion

Non-stationarity is not a nuisance to be denoised away at the edges; it is the central obstacle between a model that fit the past and a model that predicts the future. The progression in this article is the progression the field itself took. Global normalization stabilizes optimization but cannot track a moving mean, and on strongly drifting data it can underperform doing nothing at all. Per-instance reversible normalization — remove each window’s level and scale, predict the stationary shape, then restore the level — is the move that actually addresses drift, and it is cheap enough to wrap around any model. Its own limitation points to the frontier: when the level is the signal, or when the lookback and horizon distributions genuinely differ, you need methods that re-inject or separately model those statistics rather than simply removing them. Measure your series’ drift first, with a scale-free metric, and let the size and nature of that drift decide how much normalization machinery you actually need.
