Recursive, Direct, or Joint? A Comparison of Multi-Step Forecasting Strategies

How you generate an H-step forecast is a modeling decision in its own right. This article compares the recursive, direct, and joint (MIMO) strategies in terms of error propagation, the bias-variance trade-off, computational cost, and the conditions under which each one wins.

1 The question hidden inside “predict the next 24 hours”

Most introductions to time series forecasting train a model to predict a single step ahead and stop there. But real applications almost never want one value. An energy operator needs the next 24 hours of load, a retailer needs the next six weeks of demand, a clinical model needs the next several days of bed occupancy. The moment the horizon \(H\) exceeds 1, a question appears that the one-step setup never forced you to answer: how do you produce \(H\) values from a model that, fundamentally, learns a mapping from past to future?

This is not a hyperparameter detail. The strategy you pick changes which loss the model actually minimizes, how forecast errors accumulate across the horizon, how many models you have to train and store, and whether the dependency structure between \(\hat{y}_{N+1}\) and \(\hat{y}_{N+5}\) is preserved or thrown away. Three strategies dominate practice: recursive, direct, and joint (the latter usually called Multiple-Output or MIMO in the literature). They embody genuinely different assumptions, and the empirical record on which is best is famously inconsistent — @taieb2012 found contradictory conclusions across the prior literature, with some studies favoring recursive, others direct, others multi-output.

Throughout, a multi-step task means predicting the next \(H\) values \([y_{N+1}, \dots, y_{N+H}]\) of a series \([y_1, \dots, y_N]\), with \(H > 1\) the forecast horizon and \(d\) the number of past lags used as input (the embedding dimension).

2 Recursive: one model, fed its own output

The recursive (also iterated) strategy is the oldest and most intuitive. You train a single one-step model \(f\):

\[ y_{t+1} = f(y_t, y_{t-1}, \dots, y_{t-d+1}) + w_t, \tag{1}\]

where \(y_t \in \mathbb{R}\) is the value at time \(t\), the input is the vector of the \(d\) most recent lags \((y_t, \dots, y_{t-d+1}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\), \(w_t\) is the noise term, and \(f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}\) is the learned mapping. The model is trained only on the one-step objective.

To forecast \(H\) steps, you apply \(\hat{f}\) repeatedly, feeding each prediction back in as if it were an observation:

\[ \hat{y}_{N+h} = \begin{cases} \hat{f}(y_N, \dots, y_{N-d+1}) & h = 1 \\[4pt] \hat{f}(\hat{y}_{N+h-1}, \dots, \hat{y}_{N+1}, y_N, \dots, y_{N-d+h}) & 2 \le h \le d \\[4pt] \hat{f}(\hat{y}_{N+h-1}, \dots, \hat{y}_{N+h-d}) & h > d \end{cases} \tag{2}\]

The middle case is the important one: as soon as \(h \ge 2\), at least one input is itself a prediction, not an observation. By the time \(h > d\), every input is a prediction. This is where the strategy’s well-known weakness comes from — errors feed forward. A small mistake at step 1 becomes part of the input at step 2, which produces a larger mistake, and so on. The model was never trained on its own noisy outputs, so it has no chance to learn to be robust to them; the training and forecasting input distributions differ.

The theoretical statement behind this is that minimizing one-step error does not minimize \(H\)-step error, and the recursive strategy is asymptotically biased [@taieb2016class]. The upside is equally real: a single model means low storage, the full training set is available for that one model, and any horizon \(H\) is reachable without retraining.

3 Direct: one model per horizon

The direct strategy attacks the bias problem head-on by training a separate model for each step. For each \(h \in \{1, \dots, H\}\) you fit \(f_h\):

\[ y_{t+h} = f_h(y_t, y_{t-1}, \dots, y_{t-d+1}) + w_t, \tag{3}\]

so \(f_h: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}\) maps the same \(d\) observed lags directly to the value \(h\) steps ahead. Forecasts are then produced in one shot, with no feedback:

\[ \hat{y}_{N+h} = \hat{f}_h(y_N, y_{N-1}, \dots, y_{N-d+1}), \quad h = 1, \dots, H. \tag{4}\]

Because every \(\hat{f}_h\) takes only observed values as input, there is no error accumulation — the failure mode of the recursive strategy is structurally absent. Each model also optimizes exactly the objective you care about for its own horizon, which is why the direct strategy is theoretically unbiased [@taieb2016class].

The cost shows up in three places. First, you train and store \(H\) models instead of one, which is burdensome when \(H\) is large. Second, each model is trained independently, so the forecasts ignore the dependency structure across the horizon: nothing ties \(\hat{y}_{N+5}\) to \(\hat{y}_{N+4}\), which can produce jagged, mutually inconsistent trajectories. Third, the effective number of training examples shrinks for longer horizons, and being unbiased gives no guarantee of being consistent in finite samples [@taieb2016class].

4 Joint (MIMO): one model, a vector output

The joint strategy — Multi-Input Multi-Output, MIMO — trains a single model that emits the entire horizon at once. Instead of a scalar output, \(F\) returns a vector:

\[ [y_{t+1}, \dots, y_{t+H}] = F(y_t, y_{t-1}, \dots, y_{t-d+1}) + \mathbf{w}_t, \tag{5}\]

where now \(F: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^H\) maps the \(d\) input lags to an \(H\)-dimensional output vector in a single pass, and \(\mathbf{w}_t \in \mathbb{R}^H\) is a vector noise term. The forecast is simply

\[ [\hat{y}_{N+1}, \dots, \hat{y}_{N+H}] = \hat{F}(y_N, \dots, y_{N-d+1}). \tag{6}\]

Two properties follow. There is no recursion, so no error accumulation. And because all \(H\) outputs come from one model with shared parameters, the strategy preserves the stochastic dependency between forecasted values — the motivation that introduced MIMO in the first place [@taieb2012]. A neural network with an \(H\)-unit output layer, or a sequence-to-sequence decoder, is the natural modern implementation.

The trade-off is flexibility. A single shared model imposes the same functional form on every horizon, even though the relationship between the input window and \(h=1\) may differ from the relationship at \(h=H\). The intermediate DIRMO strategy was proposed precisely to interpolate here, predicting blocks of size \(s\) so that \(H/s\) models each emit an \(s\)-vector, trading off dependency preservation against per-horizon flexibility [@taieb2012].

5 Putting the trade-offs side by side

Property Recursive Direct Joint (MIMO)
Models trained 1 \(H\) 1
Output per call scalar scalar (\(\times H\)) vector \(\mathbb{R}^H\)
Error accumulation yes no no
Train/forecast input match no (uses own preds) yes yes
Cross-horizon dependency implicit ignored preserved
Per-horizon flexibility none (shared \(f\)) full none (shared \(F\))
Asymptotic bias biased unbiased depends on \(F\) class
Storage / training cost low high low

The table makes the core tension visible. Recursive and joint both use one model (cheap), but recursive pays for it with error accumulation while joint pays with inflexibility. Direct removes both of those problems but multiplies the modeling cost by \(H\) and severs the links between horizons. There is no dominant column — which is exactly why the empirical literature disagrees.

6 A reproducible comparison harness

The code below sets up an honest comparison on a single series. It is deliberately model-agnostic in spirit but uses a simple regressor so the strategy — not the learner — is what varies. The key methodological points are that the train/test split respects time order (no shuffling, no future leakage), the same lag window \(d\) and horizon \(H\) are used across strategies, and the random seed is fixed.

Code 
import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error

SEED = 42
np.random.seed(SEED)

# Synthetic series: trend + seasonality + noise.
# This is illustrative scaffolding, not a benchmark result.
N = 600
t = np.arange(N)
series = (
    0.01 * t
    + 2.0 * np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 24)
    + np.random.normal(0, 0.5, size=N)
)

D = 24   # embedding dimension (lags used as input)
H = 12   # forecast horizon
TEST = 100
Code 
def make_supervised(s, d, h):
    """Build (X, Y) where X has shape (n_samples, d) of past lags
    and Y has shape (n_samples, h) of the next h targets.
    Time order is preserved; row i never sees information after its window."""
    X, Y = [], []
    for i in range(d, len(s) - h + 1):
        X.append(s[i - d:i])          # d past observations
        Y.append(s[i:i + h])          # h future targets
    return np.array(X), np.array(y_stack := Y)

train_series = series[:-TEST]
X_tr, Y_tr = make_supervised(train_series, D, H)
# X_tr: (n_samples, D)   Y_tr: (n_samples, H)
Code 
def fit_recursive(X, y_onestep):
    """One model trained on the 1-step target (first column of Y)."""
    m = Ridge(alpha=1.0, random_state=SEED)
    m.fit(X, y_onestep)
    return m

def forecast_recursive(model, last_window, h, d):
    win = list(last_window)
    preds = []
    for _ in range(h):
        yhat = model.predict(np.array(win[-d:]).reshape(1, -1))[0]
        preds.append(yhat)
        win.append(yhat)             # feed prediction back in
    return np.array(preds)

def fit_direct(X, Y):
    """H independent models, one per horizon."""
    return [Ridge(alpha=1.0).fit(X, Y[:, h]) for h in range(Y.shape[1])]

def forecast_direct(models, last_window):
    x = last_window.reshape(1, -1)
    return np.array([m.predict(x)[0] for m in models])

def fit_joint(X, Y):
    """Single multi-output model: Ridge supports vector targets directly."""
    return Ridge(alpha=1.0).fit(X, Y)

def forecast_joint(model, last_window):
    return model.predict(last_window.reshape(1, -1))[0]
Code 
# eval is disabled here: run locally to obtain real numbers.
rec = fit_recursive(X_tr, Y_tr[:, 0])
dir_ = fit_direct(X_tr, Y_tr)
joint = fit_joint(X_tr, Y_tr)

last = train_series[-D:]
truth = series[-TEST:-TEST + H]

p_rec   = forecast_recursive(rec, last, H, D)
p_dir   = forecast_direct(dir_, last)
p_joint = forecast_joint(joint, last)

for name, p in [("recursive", p_rec), ("direct", p_dir), ("joint", p_joint)]:
    print(name, np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(truth, p)))

7 How to read the results, and what the literature actually says

The clean theoretical story — recursive is biased but low-variance, direct is unbiased but higher-variance, joint preserves dependency — is a useful guide but an unreliable predictor. Across published comparisons the rankings flip with the dataset, the base learner, and the horizon. @taieb2012, running 111 series from the NN5 competition, found multiple-output (joint-family) strategies to be the best performers overall, and also found that deseasonalizing the series before forecasting improved accuracy uniformly across strategies — a reminder that preprocessing can matter more than the strategy choice. A recent analysis revisits the bias-variance framing itself and reports that recursive strategies can match or beat direct ones in practice despite the latter looking theoretically preferable [@green2025epistemic], attributing the gap to factors the textbook decomposition omits.

The practical reading: do not select a strategy from first principles alone. Hold out a realistic validation horizon, evaluate per-step error with rolling-origin resampling, and let the data on your series decide. The rectify strategy of @taieb2016class is worth knowing as a principled middle ground — it starts from biased recursive forecasts and fits a direct correction on top, and was reported to be at least as good as the better of recursive and direct.

8 Limitations

This comparison is about strategy, holding the learner fixed; with a different base model (gradient boosting, an LSTM, a Transformer) the relative ranking can change, and some architectures blur the categories — a sequence-to-sequence model is joint by construction, while an autoregressive Transformer decoder is recursive at inference. The synthetic series used here is for illustrating the mechanics, not for drawing conclusions; any real verdict requires multiple real series and proper resampling. Error accumulation in the recursive case also interacts with the noise level and the stability of the underlying dynamics, so a single dataset cannot settle the question. Finally, none of these strategies addresses forecast uncertainty directly; quantifying predictive intervals over a horizon is a separate problem that interacts with the strategy choice in ways not covered here.

9 Conclusion

The choice among recursive, direct, and joint is a choice about which problem you are willing to have. Recursive buys cheapness and any-horizon flexibility at the price of compounding error. Direct buys unbiasedness and per-horizon tailoring at the price of \(H\) models and broken cross-horizon structure. Joint buys a single cheap model that respects the dependency across the horizon, at the price of imposing one functional form on every step. Theory ranks them only loosely; the empirical literature is openly contradictory; and the strategy that wins on your data is something you should measure, per horizon, rather than assume. If you want one default to start from, a multi-output model with proper deseasonalization is well supported by the NN5-scale evidence — but treat it as a baseline to beat, not a settled answer.

an unreliable predictor. Across published comparisons the rankings flip with the
dataset, the base learner, and the horizon. @taieb2012, running 111 series from
the NN5 competition, found multiple-output (joint-family) strategies to be the
best performers overall, and also found that deseasonalizing the series before
forecasting improved accuracy uniformly across strategies — a reminder that
preprocessing can matter more than the strategy choice. A recent analysis
revisits the bias-variance framing itself and reports that recursive strategies
can match or beat direct ones in practice despite the latter looking
theoretically preferable [@green2025epistemic], attributing the gap to factors
the textbook decomposition omits.

The practical reading: do not select a strategy from first principles alone.
Hold out a realistic validation horizon, evaluate per-step error with
rolling-origin resampling, and let the data on *your* series decide. The
**rectify** strategy of @taieb2016class is worth knowing as a principled
middle ground — it starts from biased recursive forecasts and fits a direct
correction on top, and was reported to be at least as good as the better of
recursive and direct.

## Limitations

This comparison is about *strategy*, holding the learner fixed; with a different
base model (gradient boosting, an LSTM, a Transformer) the relative ranking can
change, and some architectures blur the categories — a sequence-to-sequence
model is joint by construction, while an autoregressive Transformer decoder is
recursive at inference. The synthetic series used here is for illustrating the
mechanics, not for drawing conclusions; any real verdict requires multiple real
series and proper resampling. Error accumulation in the recursive case also
interacts with the noise level and the stability of the underlying dynamics,
so a single dataset cannot settle the question. Finally, none of these
strategies addresses forecast *uncertainty* directly; quantifying predictive
intervals over a horizon is a separate problem that interacts with the strategy
choice in ways not covered here.

## Conclusion

The choice among recursive, direct, and joint is a choice about which problem you
are willing to have. Recursive buys cheapness and any-horizon flexibility at the
price of compounding error. Direct buys unbiasedness and per-horizon tailoring at
the price of $H$ models and broken cross-horizon structure. Joint buys a single
cheap model that respects the dependency across the horizon, at the price of
imposing one functional form on every step. Theory ranks them only loosely; the
empirical literature is openly contradictory; and the strategy that wins on your
data is something you should measure, per horizon, rather than assume. If you
want one default to start from, a multi-output model with proper
deseasonalization is well supported by the NN5-scale evidence — but treat it as a
baseline to beat, not a settled answer.
